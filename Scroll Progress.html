Introduction

The quiet art of reading progress.

Scroll this page and watch the three indicators update in concert: a top bar grows from left to right, a circular dial in the corner fills with colour, and side-rail dots illuminate as each section enters view.

These small details communicate to a reader that the content has structure — that there is a beginning, a middle, and an end — and that they are progressing through it with purpose.

"Good design is as little design as possible." — Dieter Rams

The best progress indicators are invisible until you notice them, and then immediately obvious. They do not demand attention; they reward it. A thin bar at the edge of the viewport, a gentle arc completing itself — these are signals that respect the reader.

Implemented entirely in vanilla HTML, CSS and JavaScript. No libraries. No dependencies. Just a scroll event listener, a little trigonometry for the SVG circle, and an IntersectionObserver for the section dots.

The Craft

Writing is rewriting.

Every good piece of writing begins as something worse. The first draft is an act of courage — a willingness to produce something imperfect so that something better can eventually emerge from it. The blank page is not an enemy; it is an invitation.

Ernest Hemingway wrote standing up. Truman Capote wrote lying down. Maya Angelou booked hotel rooms and wrote from morning until two in the afternoon, then spent the rest of the day editing. The specifics of the ritual matter less than the commitment to it.

What every great writer shares is a willingness to sit with difficulty — to resist the urge to fill silence with sound, to let a sentence breathe before deciding whether it lives or dies.

✏️

Draft freely

Suspend judgment. Let ideas land on the page before evaluating them.

✂️

Edit ruthlessly

Kill your darlings. A sentence that does not serve the whole does not belong.

📖

Read aloud

The ear catches what the eye misses. Rhythm lives in sound, not symbols.

Design

Typography is invisible until it isn't.

The measure of good typography is its absence from conscious thought. When a reader is absorbed in text, they are not thinking about the font; they are thinking about the ideas. The moment they notice the typeface, something has gone wrong — or, occasionally, something has gone surprisingly right.

Choosing a typeface is choosing a voice. Serif type carries history, authority, and warmth. Sans-serif suggests clarity, modernity, and precision. Monospace implies code, terminals, and exactitude. These are not rules — they are tendencies, and the most interesting design decisions happen at their edges.

"The details are not the details. They make the design." — Charles Eames

Line height, letter spacing, measure (the number of characters per line) — each of these variables contributes to the invisible infrastructure that makes text either a pleasure or a chore to read. The optimal measure for body text sits between 45 and 75 characters. This paragraph is close to that.

Process

Systems thinking for the creative mind.

A creative process without structure is a storm without a course. Productive constraint is not the enemy of originality; it is often the precondition for it. The sonnet's fourteen lines, the haiku's seventeen syllables, the three-act structure of drama — these are not cages but climbing frames.

When you sit down to work without a system, you spend your best creative energy deciding what to do rather than doing it. The writer who has a morning routine, a word-count target, and a fixed revision schedule can deploy their imagination fully, because the scaffolding is already in place.

Build your creative system by observing your own rhythms. When are you sharpest? What conditions produce your best work? The answers are personal and non-transferable, but the discipline of asking them is universal.

🕐

Protect time

Block the hours when you do your best thinking before anything else claims them.

📋

Capture everything

Ideas arrive uninvited. The ones you write down survive; the others evaporate.

🔁

Review & iterate

A weekly review of your work surfaces patterns invisible in the daily grind.

Closing

The end is where you begin again.

You have reached the bottom of this page. Three indicators tracked your journey here: the bar at the top of the viewport, the ring in the lower-right corner, and the dots along the left margin.

Each one said the same thing in a different register: you are here, this is how far you have come, and the end is near. Small pieces of information, delivered at the right moment, make an experience feel intentional.

That is, ultimately, what progress indicators are about — not measurement, but reassurance. The reader who knows they are making progress is the reader who keeps reading.

"Begin at the beginning and go on till you come to the end: then stop." — Lewis Carroll
